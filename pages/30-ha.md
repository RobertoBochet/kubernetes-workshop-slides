# High Availability

An HA cluster is the foundation, but it **does not automatically guarantee** an application's high availability

<v-click>

An application deployed in a single `Pod` represents a **single point of failure** (SPOF)  
and is prone to downtime for several reasons, including:

</v-click>

<v-clicks>

- **Startup** and rescheduling phases
- **Overload** and unresponsiveness
- **Crashes** and restart cycles

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

A proper deployment design is therefore essential to guarantee high availability

</v-click>

<v-space size="xl" />

<NoteBox v-click>

This design approach also makes sense for non-HA clusters, as it helps **partially mitigate** the issues described above

</NoteBox>

# HA Stateless Application

It is the simplest scenario for achieving high availability

<v-clicks>

Resilience is achieved by replicating the workload; a `Deployment` is the ideal choice for stateless applications

A `Service` acts as a load balancer, distributing network traffic among the interchangeable `Pod` replicas

Unhealthy `Pods` are automatically excluded from traffic distribution until they are ready to serve again, or are replaced by a new instance

</v-clicks>

<NoteBox type="Warning" v-click>

HA effectiveness relies on properly configured and reactive **probes**;
an accurate `readinessProbe` is vital to ensure traffic only reaches fully operational `Pods`

</NoteBox>

# HA Stateful Application

Achieving HA while preserving state consistency is a more challenging task

<v-clicks>

Unlike stateless ones, stateful applications must be designed from the ground up to support HA

Typically, each replica requires its own dedicated storage and a **sticky network identity**; through inter-replica communication, they maintain a consistent cluster-wide state

`StatefulSets` are the standard tool for implementing this kind of architecture in Kubernetes

</v-clicks>

## [`CAP` theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAP_theorem)

In database theory, this theorem states a hard constraint on a distributed stateful system 

<v-clicks>

- `Consistency`  
    Every read operation receives the **most recent write** or an error
- `Availability`  
    Every operation receives a response without errors
- `Partition tolerance`  
    The system continues to operate despite network partitions

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

The _`CAP` theorem_ states that in a real system you can guarantee only two of these simultaneously,  
defining three types of systems (`AP`, `CP` and `CA`)

</v-click>

<v-space size="md"/>

<NoteBox v-click>

`Partition tolerance` in a real scenario is hard to be sacrificed, so the real choice is between `AP` or `CP` systems

</NoteBox>

## [`PACELC` design principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PACELC_design_principle)

This is an extension of the _`CAP` theorem_

<v-clicks>

The _`CAP` theorem_ puts the focus on system behavior when it is unhealthy, while the _`PACELC` principle_ also considers scenarios when the system is working well (hopefully 99% of the time)

The `P` branch remains equivalent to the `CAP` theorem, assuming `Partition tolerance` cannot be sacrificed

The `E` (else) branch refers to the normal operational status, where a trade-off between `Latency` and `Consistency` must be made

Therefore, this classification defines four system categories
(`PA/EL`, `PA/EC`, `PC/EL` and `PC/EC`)

</v-clicks>

## Single-Leader Replication

This is the most common model for replication of stateful services

<v-clicks>

The model introduces asymmetrical roles among replicas: one instance assumes the role of **Leader**, responsible for defining the system state and coordinating the other replicas to ensure consistency

The **Leader** role is not fixed and can change if the instance fails or is rotated by the algorithm;  
a new **Leader** is chosen via **Leader Election**, requiring a **Quorum**

A **Quorum** is the minimum number of active members (typically a majority, $\lfloor N/2 \rfloor + 1$) required to reach consensus and ensure cluster decisions are valid

Replicas periodically communicate to ensure they are part of the **Quorum**; if they lose consensus, they stop operating to prevent [split-brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-brain_(computing)) scenarios

</v-clicks>

# [_etcd_](https://etcd.io/) Case Study

etcd relies on the [_Raft_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raft_(algorithm)) consensus algorithm to ensure data integrity

<NoteBox v-click>

**Leader Election**  
The system requires one **Leader** to operate; other replicas act as **Followers**.  
An election is triggered during cluster startup or if the current **Leader** fails or loses contact with the **Quorum**.

</NoteBox>

<NoteBox v-click>

**Write Operation**  
A replica (potentially the **Leader** itself) receives the request and ensures it is handled by the **Leader**. The **Leader** appends the change to its local log and propagates it to the **Followers**; once the **Quorum** is reached, the **Leader** commits the change, propagates the update, and authorizes the initially contacted replica to confirm the operation to the user.

</NoteBox>

<NoteBox v-click>

**Read Operation (Linearizable)**  
The contacted replica asks the **Leader** for the index of the latest committed change. The **Leader** verifies its authority with the **Quorum** and responds. Once the replica ensures its local state is updated to that index, it returns the data to the user.

</NoteBox>

<NoteBox type="Success" v-click>

This strict coordination mechanism places **etcd** in the **`PC/EC`** category

</NoteBox>
